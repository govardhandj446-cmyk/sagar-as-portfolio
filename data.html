Overview

This chapter introduces the fundamental building blocks of statistics: data types, measurement scales, frequency distributions, summary statistics, and graphical tools. These concepts are essential for analyzing and communicating real-world information clearly.

Types of Data

Qualitative Data

Describes categories or labels. Examples include gender, color, or type of product.

Quantitative Data

Represents numerical values. Examples include height, weight, marks, or temperature.

Discrete Data

Countable values, usually whole numbers. Example: number of students attending class.

Continuous Data

Measurable values that can include decimals. Example: time, distance, or temperature.

Scales of Measurement

Nominal

Labels without order, such as car brands or city names.

Ordinal

Ordered categories where spacing is not equal, such as class ranks or satisfaction levels.

Interval

Ordered values with equal intervals but no true zero, like temperature in Celsius.

Ratio

Ordered values with equal intervals and a true zero, like weight or distance.

Frequency Distribution

A frequency distribution shows how often each value or range of values occurs in a dataset. It helps identify patterns and summarise large datasets cleanly.

Example: Discrete Distribution

Marks vs Frequency

  • 1 → 2
  • 2 → 5
  • 3 → 3

Example: Continuous Distribution

Use intervals for measured data:

  • 0–10 → 4
  • 10–20 → 6
  • 20–30 → 5

Summary Statistics

Mean

The average of all values: mean = sum of observations / number of observations.

Median

The middle value when data is ordered. If there is an even number of values, the median is the average of the two middle values.

Mode

The value that appears most often in the dataset.

Range

The difference between the maximum and minimum values.

Dispersion & Spread

Variance and standard deviation measure how much values differ from the mean. The coefficient of variation expresses variability relative to the mean.

Visualization Tools

Visual tools make data easier to understand at a glance.

  • Histogram — Shows the distribution of values across intervals.
  • Boxplot — Visualises the median, quartiles, and outliers.
  • Frequency Table — Lists counts for values or intervals.

    • Practical Example

    Suppose the marks of 10 students are 75, 89, 66, 52, 90, 68, 83, 94, 77, 60.

    Then:

    • Mean = 75.4
    • Median = 76
    • Mode = no repeated values in this dataset
    • Range = 94 - 52 = 42

Why It Matters

Good data description is the first step in any statistical analysis. It allows us to understand the dataset, spot errors, choose the right methods, and communicate findings effectively.
